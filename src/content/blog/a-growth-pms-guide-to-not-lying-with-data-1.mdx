# A Growth PM's Guide to Not Lying With Data

Most growth PMs treat A/B testing like a vending machine. Set up the experiment, wait a few days, check if the dashboard says green or red, ship or kill. The math in between? That's the data scientist's job.

This is a problem. Not because PMs need to become statisticians, but because the decisions — what to ship, what to kill, what to invest a quarter into building properly — are PM decisions. And right now, most PMs are making those calls without understanding what their tests actually told them.

Growth culture celebrates velocity. "Test everything" is the mantra. But "test everything" without understanding the math is just "guess everything with extra steps."

Here's the thing: the math behind A/B testing is not a PhD-level problem. You learned everything you need in school — means, distributions, probability. What makes it feel inaccessible is the vocabulary. P-values, confidence intervals, statistical power — these words do a lot of gatekeeping for concepts that are, at their core, simple. This post is my attempt to strip away the jargon and get to what actually matters.

This post covers three concepts that form a trilogy. Statistical significance tells you *if* something happened. Confidence intervals tell you *how much*. And minimum detectable effect tells you *whether you could have known at all*. Together, they're the difference between learning from a test and lying with one.

The cost of getting this wrong isn't abstract. It's bad features shipped to millions of users. Good features killed because a test was too small to detect them. Entire quarters burned on experiments that were doomed before they launched.

## The Courtroom Analogy: What Statistical Significance Actually Means

In a courtroom, the defendant is innocent until proven guilty. The prosecution has to bring enough evidence to overcome that default. The defense doesn't have to prove innocence — the burden sits entirely on the other side.

A/B testing works the same way.

The null hypothesis is your defendant. It says: "there is no difference between control and variant." That's the default. Your test is the prosecution — it needs to bring enough evidence to reject that default. Until it does, "no difference" stands.

This is where p-values come in, and where most PMs get it wrong.

A p-value of 0.04 does *not* mean "there's a 96% chance the variant is better." This is the single most common misinterpretation I see, and it changes decisions. What p=0.04 actually means: if there were truly no difference between control and variant, you'd see results this extreme only 4% of the time. It's a statement about the evidence, not about the truth.

<Callout>
**The p-value trap:** p=0.04 means "this evidence would be unlikely if nothing changed." It does *not* mean "there's a 96% chance something changed." One is about the data. The other is about the world. PMs confuse the two constantly.
</Callout>

Think about the courtroom again. The prosecutor presents a strong case. That doesn't mean the defendant is guilty. It means the evidence would be unlikely if they were innocent. Those are different statements, and confusing them is how PMs overstate confidence in test results.

The 0.05 threshold everyone uses? It's a convention. Not a law of physics. It's the statistical equivalent of "beyond reasonable doubt" — a socially agreed-upon line that Fisher proposed and the industry adopted. There's nothing magical about it. A p-value of 0.049 and 0.051 are practically identical, but one gets shipped and the other gets killed.

This matters because misreading p-values has a direct cost. It makes PMs say "we're 95% confident this works" in a review when what the data actually supports is far more nuanced.

Let's make this concrete. You run a test. 10,000 users per variant. Conversion rate goes from 5.0% in control to 5.3% in the variant. Your tool says p=0.03. What do you actually know?

You know that a difference this large would be unlikely to appear by chance alone — only a 3% probability if the null hypothesis were true. That's it. You don't know the *true* size of the effect. You don't know if 0.3 percentage points matters for your business. And you don't know whether your test was well-designed enough to trust in the first place.

With enough users, even a completely meaningless 0.01% lift becomes "statistically significant." Significance is not importance. It's a filter for noise, not a measure of value.

Two more courtroom parallels worth knowing. A Type I error — false positive — is convicting an innocent person. Your test says there's a difference when there isn't one. This is what the 0.05 threshold controls: you're accepting a 5% chance of this happening. A Type II error — false negative — is letting a guilty person walk free. Your test misses a real difference. This one is less discussed but equally dangerous, and it connects directly to statistical power, which we'll touch on when we get to MDE.

Statistical significance answers one question: *did something happen?* It says nothing about *how much* happened. For that, you need confidence intervals.

## Confidence Intervals: The Range You're Actually Betting On

Statistical significance gives you a yes or no. Your stakeholders ask "by how much?" The confidence interval is the honest answer.

If significance is "did the arrow hit the target?", the confidence interval is "where on the target did it land, and how wobbly was the archer?" A tight grouping means you know something precise. A scatter across the board means you got lucky.

A 95% confidence interval means this: if you ran the same experiment 100 times, about 95 of those intervals would contain the true effect. It's a statement about the *method's* reliability across repeated experiments, not a 95% probability that any single interval contains the truth. The distinction is subtle, and in practice most PMs can treat it as "the range of plausible effects." But knowing the precise definition keeps you from overstating your certainty in a review.

Here's why CIs matter more than p-values for PM decisions: they give you a range of plausible business impact, not just a binary signal.

Let's continue the example from the last section. Your conversion rate went from 5.0% to 5.3%, p=0.03. Sounds like a win. Now look at the confidence interval: [+0.05%, +0.55%]. The true lift could be anywhere in that range. At +0.55%, this is a meaningful feature worth investing in. At +0.05%, it's noise that happens to be statistically significant. Same test, same p-value — completely different decisions depending on where in the CI the truth sits.

<Callout>
**Same p-value, opposite decisions.** A CI of [+0.05%, +0.55%] means the truth could be "invest heavily" or "not worth the code complexity." The p-value can't tell you which. The CI can.
</Callout>

The width of the interval tells you how much you actually learned. A narrow CI means the test gave you a sharp, usable answer. A wide CI means you ran an experiment and came out the other side barely smarter than when you started. Width is a function of sample size — more users, narrower interval, sharper answer.

When the CI includes zero, your test didn't clear significance. The range of plausible effects includes "no difference at all." This is the mathematical equivalent of the jury saying "we're not sure."

Most experimentation dashboards display confidence intervals. Most PMs skip straight to the green or red badge. This is like reading only the headline of an article and making a decision based on it. The CI *is* the article.

CIs also give you a better vocabulary for stakeholder conversations. "We saw a 5.3% conversion rate" sounds precise but isn't. "We saw a lift somewhere between 0.05% and 0.55%" is less satisfying but far more honest. And when someone wants to ship a marginal result, pointing to a wide confidence interval is more persuasive than arguing about p-values. It makes the uncertainty visible.

Back to the courtroom. If the p-value is the verdict — guilty or not guilty — the confidence interval is the sentencing range. "Guilty, sentenced to somewhere between 2 and 20 years." The jury was sure enough that *something* happened, but deeply uncertain about how serious it was. A PM who ships based on significance alone is a judge who hears "guilty" and doesn't bother asking how many years.

Confidence intervals tell you how much happened, and how precisely you know it. But here's the catch: CI width is something you *discover after* the test. Minimum detectable effect is how you *plan for* the right precision before you run it.

## Minimum Detectable Effect: The Question You Forgot to Ask

Most PMs skip this concept entirely. It's the least discussed of the three, and arguably the most important.

MDE is the smallest lift your test can reliably detect, given your sample size, baseline conversion rate, and significance threshold. Below that threshold, your test is blind. It doesn't matter how long you run it or how carefully you read the results. If the real effect is smaller than your MDE, your experiment will never see it.

Think of it as a flashlight. You're searching for something in the dark. If the thing you're looking for is 200 meters away and your flashlight reaches 50 meters, standing there longer doesn't help. You don't need more patience. You need a stronger light.

<Callout>
**The flashlight rule:** If your test's MDE is 15% but the real effect is 3%, no amount of waiting will help. You need more traffic, a bigger intervention, or a different validation method entirely.
</Callout>

This connects to a concept called statistical power — the probability that your test will detect a real effect when one exists. The industry standard is 80% power, meaning you accept a 20% chance of missing a real effect. MDE is the effect size at which your test hits that power level. Below it, you're mostly guessing.

Here's how this plays out in practice. A PM runs a test on a low-traffic surface. The test runs for six weeks. The result comes back inconclusive. The PM concludes "the feature doesn't work" and moves on. But the test was never powered to detect anything smaller than a 15% lift. The feature might work perfectly well — a steady 3-4% improvement — and the test simply couldn't see it.

The cost: weeks of engineering to build the variant, weeks of waiting for results, an inconclusive outcome, and a quarter burned. All of it avoidable with fifteen minutes of math before the test launched.

The math itself isn't something you need to derive from scratch. Every major experimentation platform has a sample size calculator. You plug in your baseline conversion rate, the smallest lift you care about, your significance level (usually 0.05), and your desired power (usually 0.80). It tells you how many users you need. That number, combined with your traffic, tells you how long the test will take.

This is where MDE becomes a negotiation tool. It forces a conversation between the PM, the data scientist, and engineering. "We can detect a 5% lift in two weeks, or a 2% lift in eight weeks. Which question do we want to answer?" That's a product decision, not a statistics decision. MDE makes it explicit.

Sometimes the math tells you not to test at all. If your surface gets 500 users per week and you need to detect a 3% lift, the calculator might say you need six months of data. That's not a test — that's a prayer. It's a signal to find a higher-traffic surface, a bigger intervention, or a different way to validate the idea entirely.

MDE is also a bullshit filter. If someone claims a test "proved" a 0.5% lift on a surface with 1,000 users per week after a two-week test, you can run the MDE math backward and see that the test wasn't capable of detecting an effect that small. The result isn't a finding. It's noise that got lucky.

This connects back to confidence intervals. A well-powered test — one with a low MDE relative to the true effect — produces narrow confidence intervals. You come out of the test with a sharp, usable answer. An underpowered test produces wide intervals, the kind where the true effect could be anything from barely positive to meaningfully negative. Planning your MDE upfront is how you guarantee useful confidence intervals afterward.

If you take one thing from this section: asking "what's our MDE?" before a test launches is the single highest-signal question a PM can ask in an experimentation review. It separates PMs who understand testing from PMs who are along for the ride.

Now that you understand all three concepts — significance, confidence intervals, and MDE — let's look at what happens when PMs ignore them.

## The Three Mistakes

Every growth PM makes these mistakes at least once. They're not rookie errors. They're rational-seeming instincts that happen to collide with how statistics actually works.

### Peeking

A PM on a team I worked with launched a test that added a new entry point to a feature. The next day, he checked the dashboard. The numbers looked good — usage was up. He wanted to ship immediately.

The data scientist pushed back. Run it for at least a week, she said. But the PM had his data. The lift was right there on the screen. The test got called after one day and the feature was rolled out.

The lift held for about two weeks. Then it started fading. What looked like a real improvement turned out to be a novelty effect — users clicking on something new because it was new, not because it was better. The feature underperformed in production.

Peeking is seductive because every time you check, there's a decent chance the data looks significant purely by chance. The more times you look, the more you inflate your false positive rate. If you check every day for a week at a 0.05 significance level, your actual false positive rate is far higher than 5%. The math changes when you look multiple times. Most PMs don't realize this.

The fix isn't just discipline. If you genuinely need to monitor results in real-time — say, to catch a variant that's actively hurting users — sequential testing methods are designed for exactly that. They adjust the significance threshold at each look to maintain the overall error rate. Peeking at a standard test and applying sequential logic in your head is not the same thing.

### Ignoring MDE

This one is quieter. Peeking is dramatic — a PM overrides a data scientist, ships too early, the feature flops. Ignoring MDE is invisible. Nobody notices the test that was doomed from the start. It just silently wastes a quarter.

The pattern: a PM runs a test on a low-traffic surface. The test runs for weeks. The result comes back inconclusive. The PM concludes the feature doesn't work and moves on to the next idea. But the test was never powered to detect the kind of lift the feature was realistically going to produce. A 3% improvement on a surface with 2,000 weekly users needs months of data, not weeks. The feature might be working. The test just couldn't see it.

The fix is simple and takes fifteen minutes. Before you launch, plug your baseline conversion rate, your traffic, and the smallest lift you'd care about into a sample size calculator. If the required timeline is unreasonable, change the plan. Find a higher-traffic surface. Make a bigger intervention. Or accept that this isn't something you can A/B test and find another way to validate it.

The saddest outcome isn't a failed test. It's a good idea killed by an underpowered one.

### Misreading P-Values

You already know this one from the courtroom section. But it's worth seeing how it plays out in a meeting room.

A PM presents results to leadership: "We're 96% confident the variant is better." The room nods. Roadmaps shift. Resources get allocated. Nobody in the room catches it because the mistake sounds like confidence.

The PM stated a probability about the world. The p-value is a probability about the data. The gap between those two claims is where bad decisions live.

And it compounds. One misread result informs the next test, which informs the next prioritization, which shapes the roadmap. A single misinterpretation doesn't just affect one feature — it biases the entire learning loop.

### The Root Cause

All three mistakes share a common thread: treating the experimentation platform as a black box. The dashboard shows green, you ship. It shows red, you kill. The math in between never gets examined.

The antidote isn't becoming a statistician. It's understanding enough to ask the right questions — before the test launches, while it's running, and when you're reading the results.

## A PM's Pre-Launch Checklist

Everything above distills into five questions. Ask them before every test.

**1. What's the baseline?**
Know your current conversion rate, engagement metric, or whatever you're measuring. You can't detect a change if you don't know where you started. This sounds obvious. It gets skipped more often than you'd think.

**2. What's the smallest effect that would matter to the business?**
Not the smallest effect you can detect — the smallest one that would justify shipping the feature and investing further. A 0.1% lift on a checkout page might move millions in revenue. A 0.1% lift on an onboarding tooltip might not be worth the code complexity. Define what "worth it" means before the data arrives.

**3. Do we have enough traffic to detect that effect?**
Run the sample size calculation. Plug in your baseline, your minimum meaningful lift, 0.05 significance, 0.80 power. The calculator gives you a number. Divide by your weekly traffic. That's how long your test needs to run. If the answer is longer than you have patience or roadmap space for, change the plan before you start.

**4. How long will this test need to run?**
Even if the sample size math says "four days," run it for at least a full week. User behavior varies by day of week. Novelty effects inflate early results. Seasonality matters. The sample size calculator tells you the *minimum*. Real-world noise often demands more.

**5. What will we do with each possible outcome?**
Define your actions before the results come in. If significant and positive — ship. If significant and negative — kill. If inconclusive — what's the next step? Run a follow-up? Change the variant? Move on? Deciding this upfront prevents post-hoc rationalization, which is the quiet cousin of all three mistakes above.

Most PMs do some version of questions 1 and 5 intuitively. Questions 2, 3, and 4 — the math questions — are the ones that get skipped. They take fifteen to thirty minutes. They save weeks of ambiguity.

## The Honest Growth PM

Growth PM culture has a bias toward action. Ship fast, test everything, iterate. That instinct is mostly right. But velocity without comprehension isn't speed — it's just expensive guessing.

The math behind A/B testing exists to protect you from yourself — from the pattern-matching instincts that see signal in noise, from the urgency that ships before the data is ready, from the confidence that overstates what a p-value actually proved.

None of this requires you to become a statistician. It requires you to stop treating your experimentation platform like a vending machine and start treating it like what it is: a tool that gives you exactly as much as you put into understanding it.

Significance tells you if. Confidence intervals tell you how much. MDE tells you whether you could have known at all. Together, they're the difference between a PM who runs tests and a PM who learns from them.
